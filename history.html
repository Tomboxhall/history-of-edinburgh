History of Edinburgh

Edinburgh's history is a fascinating tapestry of royalty, innovation, and culture.

Edinburgh through the Ages

Medieval Times

During the medieval era, Edinburgh was a bustling hub of trade and power. Its historic Old Town, marked by narrow, winding streets, thrived as merchants and knights mingled. Edinburgh Castle, a formidable fortress, played a crucial role in the city's defense and governance, shaping its rich medieval history.

16/17th Century

In the 16th and 17th centuries, Edinburgh underwent significant change. The Scottish Reformation brought religious transformation, while the Union of the Crowns with England in 1603 united the kingdoms under James VI. The city's intellectual and cultural influence grew, with luminaries like philosopher David Hume leaving a lasting impact.

18th Century

The 18th century in Edinburgh was marked by the Enlightenment, with the city earning the nickname "Athens of the North." Intellectual luminaries like David Hume and Adam Smith thrived, and the New Town was designed, reflecting a neoclassical aesthetic. The Royal Mile remained a historic heart of the city.

19th Century

Edinburgh in the 19th century saw continued intellectual growth, with the founding of the Royal Society of Edinburgh and the construction of iconic landmarks like the Scott Monument and Waverley Station. The city became a center for medicine and education, with the establishment of the Edinburgh Medical School.
